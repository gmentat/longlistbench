LongListBench\footnote{Code, data, and implementation details are available at \url{https://github.com/kaydotai/longlistbench}.} is a synthetic benchmark for long-list entity extraction in semi-structured business documents. Each instance consists of (i) structured JSON ground truth, (ii) rendered HTML and PDF artifacts, (iii) an OCR transcript in Markdown, and (iv) metadata describing target counts, artifact paths, and evidence patterns.

\subsection{Entity schemas}
The released corpus contains three broad target families.

\paragraph{Claim incidents.}
Claim-oriented documents use a loss-run incident schema with claim identifiers, policy metadata, claimant and driver fields, loss dates, coverage fields, status fields, and nested financial breakdowns. These records are scored with stricter incident normalization, including date normalization, claimant-list sorting, and monetary rounding.

\paragraph{Policy records.}
Policy-oriented documents use heterogeneous records for locations, coverages, forms, endorsements, exclusions, rating rows, and premium items. A policy packet is the contract document issued by an insurer; it combines declarations, covered locations or classifications, coverage limits and deductibles, rating or premium schedules, required forms, and endorsements that modify the base policy. The released policy packets cover Businessowners Policy (BOP), Workers Compensation (WC), and Commercial General Liability (CGL).

\paragraph{Operations records.}
The core operations documents contain dense repeated lists from business operations workflows, including mileage rows, multi-section return rows, tax inquiry rows, driver records, vehicle schedule rows, and external loss-run rows. These record types are evaluated as generic field-value records rather than forced into the claim incident schema.

\subsection{Document design and provenance}
Private production PDFs were reviewed to identify recurring document structures, such as portal exports, spreadsheet schedules, carrier loss-run tables, policy declarations, forms, endorsements, and long packet layouts. Those private files are not included in the repository, and the released documents are production-inspired rather than facsimiles of any single source packet.

\Cref{fig:pipeline} summarizes how the corpus was built. In production, data extracted from raw customer PDFs is reviewed and corrected by humans; the recurring corrections surface the edge cases where extraction actually fails. Engineers studied those edge cases alongside the source documents and distilled them into generation scripts: per-family templates, seeded value generators, and the stressor taxonomy of \Cref{tab:complexity-stressors}. No production data leaves this step --- only the structural findings do. The scripts then produce the release: they first emit the structured target records (every name, identifier, date, value, and prose passage is generated, so no customer-sensitive data exists in the release), then render those records through HTML templates --- where the stressors are physically injected --- to PDF via headless Chromium, and finally transcribe the pages with vision OCR into the released text condition (\Cref{subsec:ocr-condition}). Because each released document is rendered from its own ground-truth records, labels are correct by construction and require no human annotation. The claim and policy multi-hop packets are generated end-to-end by the released code with recorded seeds; the 30 core-operations PDFs come from an earlier generation batch of the same design.

Each instance carries explicit regime metadata in the manifest and Hugging Face export. The \texttt{complexity\_regime} field records the high-level regime or document family, while the \texttt{problems} field records cross-cutting stressors. This separation is important: the same PDF may be both a large-document case, a multi-table case, an OCR-conditioned case, and a long-range evidence case. These regimes are intentionally not a single difficulty ladder. Many core operations files primarily test scale and completeness; deterministic parsers may perform well when OCR preserves row boundaries. The multisection IFTA packets within core operations add OCR-layout and cross-section join stressors. Claim and policy packets test different complexity: inherited context, distant evidence, mixed layouts, heterogeneous schemas, and distractor sections. Results should therefore be reported by regime rather than only as one aggregate score.

\Cref{tab:complexity-stressors} lists the canonical cross-cutting stressors. The earlier generator included simple pagination stressors; in the current release we separate those from harder item-level splits. A page break by itself mainly tests pagination and list completeness. The harder case is a split record, where fields for one target item must be assembled from separate visual sections or pages. Duplicate pressure likewise appears mainly as prior-term, archived, or near-duplicate distractor material rather than exact duplicate target rows. Per-sample metadata also includes finer domain-specific tags, such as policy-line or endorsement tags, for audit and slicing; those tags are not separate canonical stressor categories.

\subsection{OCR condition}
\label{subsec:ocr-condition}
Every released PDF has an OCR transcript generated from rendered page images. The released transcript condition was produced with Google Gemini 3.5 Flash vision OCR through the direct Google/Vertex Gemini API path, after rasterizing PDF pages at 200 DPI. The OCR transcript is the released text condition for this version; it is not the PDF text layer or the HTML source text. We treat OCR as a controlled input condition: a system may evaluate directly from the PDF, from the OCR transcript, or from its own OCR pipeline, but reported results should state the input condition clearly.

\subsection{Long-range evidence}
LongListBench includes single-document multi-hop cases because many production tasks are not contained in one adjacent table region. A target loss-run record may require joining a front claim schedule against policy registers, driver rosters, cause-code appendices, claimant indexes, and payment ledgers that appear dozens or hundreds of pages later. Policy packets require similar joins across declarations, location schedules, forms, endorsements, rating pages, and premium summaries.

This design is intended to stress extraction systems that can inspect long documents, preserve join keys across distant sections, and verify record completeness. It is also intended to make future agentic and retrieval-loop methods measurable without changing the public data format: each sample remains one PDF with one ground-truth list.
