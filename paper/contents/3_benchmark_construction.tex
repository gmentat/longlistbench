LongListBench\footnote{Code, data, and implementation details are available at \url{https://github.com/kaydotai/longlistbench}.} is a synthetic benchmark for long-list entity extraction in semi-structured business documents. Each instance consists of (i) structured JSON ground truth, (ii) rendered HTML and PDF artifacts, (iii) an OCR transcript in Markdown, and (iv) metadata describing target counts, artifact paths, and evidence patterns.

\subsection{Entity schemas}
The released corpus contains three broad target families.

\paragraph{Claim incidents.}
Claim-oriented documents use a loss-run incident schema with claim identifiers, policy metadata, claimant and driver fields, loss dates, coverage fields, status fields, and nested financial breakdowns. These records are scored with stricter incident normalization, including date normalization, claimant-list sorting, and monetary rounding.

\paragraph{Policy records.}
Policy-oriented documents use heterogeneous records for locations, coverages, forms, endorsements, exclusions, rating rows, and premium items. A policy packet is the contract document issued by an insurer; it combines declarations, covered locations or classifications, coverage limits and deductibles, rating or premium schedules, required forms, and endorsements that modify the base policy. The released policy packets cover Businessowners Policy (BOP), Workers Compensation (WC), and Commercial General Liability (CGL).

\paragraph{Operations records.}
The core operations documents contain dense lists from insurance and trucking workflows, including IFTA mileage rows, multisection IFTA return rows, IFTA return rows, tax inquiry rows, driver records, vehicle schedule rows, and external loss-run rows. These record types are evaluated as generic field-value records rather than forced into the claim incident schema.

\subsection{Document design and provenance}
\Cref{fig:generation-pipeline} illustrates the benchmark construction pipeline. Private production PDFs were reviewed to identify recurring document structures, such as portal exports, spreadsheet schedules, carrier loss-run tables, policy declarations, forms, endorsements, and long packet layouts. Those private files are not included in the repository. The released documents use generated names, identifiers, dates, values, and prose, and the repository contains only synthetic artifacts. The documents are production-inspired rather than facsimiles of any single source packet. Some sections combine realistic business-document conventions with deliberately selected stressors, such as sparse schedules, repeated keys, multi-section tables, and long-range joins, to ensure coverage of the benchmark's complexity taxonomy.

The current release prioritizes fewer, larger, more realistic PDFs rather than many small template variants. This choice reflects the intended use case: measuring list-completeness failures at scale. Several PDFs contain more than 1{,}000 target records, and the full corpus contains 33{,}450 target records across 36 PDFs.

The \texttt{complexity\_regime} field records the high-level regime or document family, while the \texttt{problems} field records cross-cutting stressors. This separation is important: the same PDF may be both a large-document case, a multi-table case, an OCR-conditioned case, and a long-range evidence case. These regimes are intentionally not a single difficulty ladder. Many core operations files primarily test scale and completeness; deterministic parsers may perform well when OCR preserves row boundaries. The multisection IFTA packets within core operations add OCR-layout and cross-section join stressors. Claim and policy packets test different complexity: inherited context, distant evidence, mixed layouts, heterogeneous schemas, and distractor sections. Results should therefore be reported by regime rather than only as one aggregate score.

\Cref{tab:complexity-stressors} lists the cross-cutting stressors. The first seven originate from the earlier generator's problem taxonomy. In the current release they are implemented in more production-like layouts rather than as visibly synthetic toggles. For example, page breaks appear as page-spanning sections with repeated headers; duplicate pressure appears mainly as prior-term, archived, or near-duplicate distractor material rather than exact duplicate target rows.

\subsection{OCR condition}
Every released PDF has an OCR transcript generated from rendered page images. The OCR transcript is the released text condition for this version. We treat OCR as a controlled input condition: a system may evaluate directly from the PDF, from the OCR transcript, or from its own OCR pipeline, but reported results should state the input condition clearly.

\subsection{Long-range evidence}
LongListBench includes single-document multi-hop cases because many production tasks are not contained in one adjacent table region. A target loss-run record may require joining a front claim schedule against policy registers, driver rosters, cause-code appendices, claimant indexes, and payment ledgers that appear dozens or hundreds of pages later. Policy packets require similar joins across declarations, location schedules, forms, endorsements, rating pages, and premium summaries.

This design is intended to stress extraction systems that can inspect long documents, preserve join keys across distant sections, and verify record completeness. It is also intended to make future agentic and retrieval-loop methods measurable without changing the public data format: each sample remains one PDF with one ground-truth list.
